## The “Draw It in One Stroke” Problem

- For a graph consisting of V vertices and E edges (or bridges), if we want to draw it in a single stroke, that is, traverse each edge exactly once, then every intermediate vertex must have even degree: every time we enter such a vertex, we must also leave it. Only the start and end vertices may have odd degree. Therefore, for a graph with n edges, **at most two vertices may have odd degree** if we want an Euler path.
- The figure below is the Seven Bridges of Königsberg problem. Since all vertices have odd degree, it is impossible.

![](https://i.postimg.cc/jjzTHdhv/IMG-20251028113611968.png)

**Euler’s Theorem**: for a connected graph, if there are at most two vertices of odd degree, then the graph has an Euler path.

- If an Euler path is not open-ended, but instead starts and ends at the same vertex, then it is called an **Euler circuit**.

**Euler’s Theorem (continued)**: for a connected graph, if every vertex has even degree, then the graph has an Euler circuit.

- Intuitively: in an Euler path, only the starting and ending vertices may have odd degree. If there is no distinct start and end, then every vertex must have even degree. Such a closed Euler path is precisely an Euler circuit.

## Basic Graph Theory Concepts

- **Degree** deg(v): the number of edges incident to a vertex v. A loop counts twice. For instance, in the Seven Bridges picture above, the leftmost vertex has degree 5.
- **Simple graph**: a graph with no multiple edges between the same two vertices, no direction on edges, and no loops.
- **Multigraph**: edges are undirected, but multiple edges and loops are allowed.
- **Directed graph**: edges have directions, and multiple edges or loops may also be allowed.
- **Neighborhood** N(v): the set of vertices adjacent to v.
- **Degree sequence**: the list of degrees of all vertices, such as “(deg(a), deg(b), …)”.
- **Isomorphic graphs**: graphs that are equivalent up to relabeling or deformation. Isomorphic graphs must have the same degree sequence.
- **Tree**: a connected graph with no cycles. Its **leaves** are the vertices with deg(v)=1.

<br>

**Handshake Theorem**: for any connected graph,
$2E = \sum_{v∈V} deg(v)$.
Therefore:

- a graph whose degree sum is odd cannot exist;
- the number of odd-degree vertices must always be even.

## Matchings and Complete Graphs

We usually denote the number of vertices in a simple graph by n, the number of edges by m, and a matching by M.

- **Matching**: a set of edges no two of which share a common endpoint. Equivalently, every vertex has degree at most 1 in the matching.
- **Maximum matching**: a matching with the largest possible number of edges.
- **Berge’s Lemma**: a matching M is maximum **iff** there is no **augmenting path** relative to M.
- **Perfect matching**: a matching that covers all vertices, so every vertex has degree 1. Necessarily, n must be even.
- **Complete graph** K<sub>n</sub>: a graph in which every vertex is adjacent to every other vertex, so every vertex has degree n−1.

How many perfect matchings does an even complete graph K<sub>2n</sub> have?  
The first vertex has 2n−1 possible partners. After that pair is fixed, the next unused vertex has 2n−3 choices, and so on, until the final two vertices are forced. Hence:
$$(2n-1)!!\ =\prod_{ß=0}^{ß=n-1}(2n-1-2ß)={(2n)!\over 2^nn!}$$

## Bipartite Graphs and Hall’s Marriage Theorem

A bipartite graph K<sub>m, n</sub> consists of two vertex sets of sizes m and n, with no edges inside the same part.

**Euler’s theorem for bipartite graphs:**

- K<sub>m, n</sub> has an Euler circuit **iff** both m and n are even.
- K<sub>m, n</sub> has an Euler path **iff** either both m and n are even, or one of them is 2 and the other is odd, or m = n = 1.

<br>

### Hall’s Theorem (for deciding whether a bipartite graph has a perfect matching)

For a bipartite graph G=(X,Y,E), there exists a matching that covers X **iff** for every subset S⊆X, we have |N(S)| ≥ |S|. In words, every subset of X must collectively have at least as many neighbors in Y as its own size.
- To prove existence: check all subsets.
- To disprove existence: give a counterexample subset S with |N(S)|<|S|.

Example:  
 U = {u1, u2, u3, u4}, V = {v1, v2, v3, v4};  
 N(u1) = {v1, v2}, N(u2) = {v1, v3}, N(u3) = {v2, v4}, N(u4) = {v3, v4}.

- |S|=1: clearly |N(S)| = 2 ≥ 1
- |S|=2: for example, |N({u1, u2})| = 3 and |N({u3, u4})| = 3; every other two-element subset also has at least 2 neighbors
- |S|=3: for example, |N({u1, u2, u3})| = 4; similarly for all other three-element subsets
- |S|=4: N(U)=V, so |N(U)|=4 ≥ 4  
Hence Hall’s condition holds, so a perfect matching exists.

## Coloring Problems

In a graph coloring problem, adjacent vertices must receive different colors. If χ(G) = k, then the graph requires at least k colors. We say that G is **k-colorable**.

- For a cycle C<sub>n</sub> (where every vertex has degree 2), we have χ(odd cycle) = 3 and χ(even cycle) = 2.
- For the complete graph K<sub>n</sub>, χ(K<sub>n</sub>) = n. Hence every graph satisfies
  $χ(G) ≥ \omega(G)$,
  where \omega(G) is the size of the largest complete subgraph.
  A special case: for every **interval graph**, the chromatic number equals the size of its largest clique.
  For example, in the interval graph of flight arrivals below, once we convert it into a “flight conflict graph,” the maximum clique is K<sub>3</sub>, so the minimum number of gates required is 3.

![](https://i.postimg.cc/5jWCPSVV/jie-ping2025-11-04-11-44-24.png)

- Every bipartite graph contains **no odd cycle**, and is therefore 2-colorable. Conversely, any graph containing an odd cycle cannot be 2-colored.
- Every planar map is 4-colorable. (Think of each country as a vertex, and shared borders as edges.)

## Planarity

**A planar graph is one that can be drawn on the plane so that no two edges cross.**

- A complete graph on at least five vertices is not planar. (As the figure below suggests, some edges must cross.)

![](https://i.postimg.cc/fRSDbvS3/jie-ping2025-10-28-17-17-07.png)



### Euler’s Formula for Planar Graphs
- A **face** is a connected region determined by the edges, including both interior faces and the exterior face. Interior faces are bounded regions enclosed by at least three edges, while the exterior face is the unbounded region outside the graph. For example, f(K<sub>4</sub>)=5.
- **Euler’s formula (planar version)**: if a **connected** planar graph has n vertices, m edges, and f faces, then:
  **n – m + f = 2**.

<br>

### Edge-Length Consequence for Simple Planar Graphs

Because a simple graph has no multiple edges between the same two vertices, every face has boundary length at least 3. Also, each edge can contribute to at most 2 face-boundaries. Therefore:
**2m ≥ 3f**.
- Intuitively: each edge contributes to at most 2 boundary-sides on the left-hand side, while each face contributes at least 3 boundary-sides on the right-hand side.

Combining this with Euler’s formula, we obtain **Euler’s inequality**:
**m ≤ 3n-6**.
Using the Handshake Theorem in the form
average degree = ∑deg(v)/n = 2m/n,
we get:
$ar{d} =  {2m\over n} ≤ {6n-12\over n} = 6- {12\over n}$.
No matter what n is, **the average degree of a simple planar graph is less than 6**, so at least one vertex must have degree at most 5.

- From this we can prove that **every planar graph is 6-colorable** by induction. A graph G must contain a vertex v of degree at most 5. If G-v is 6-colorable, then G is also 6-colorable.
