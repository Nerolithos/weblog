## The History of Networks and Computers

The 1940s were the age of the command line. ENIAC, the first general-purpose fully electronic computer, was created in 1945 at the University of Pennsylvania. At that time, users entered one command at a time, and the computer executed one command at a time.

The 1950s were the era of batch processing. Operators loaded users’ programs and data onto punched cards and magnetic tapes, and the computer read instructions in sequence. Only one program could run at a time.

The 1960s were the age of time-sharing systems. The devices used by clients were **terminals**, which had very limited computing power. They served mainly for input and output, while computation and data processing were handled by a central **host** computer connected over a network. Multiple terminals could connect to a single machine, allowing multiple users to share one system simultaneously. These private networks mainly existed to share computational power, and different hosts were usually not interconnected. During the same period, however, ARPANET — the predecessor of the Internet — was born within the U.S. Department of Defense’s ARPA.

The 1970s and 1980s saw the rise of networking. Personal computers (PCs) became widespread, and the traditional distinction between host and terminal gradually blurred, because end users could now compute locally on their own machines, such as desktop computers. This is also why the term “host” can be ambiguous today: in a PC setting, the machine itself may be referred to as the **localhost**. In 1983, ARPANET switched to the TCP/IP protocol suite, marking the formal birth of the Internet. In 1984, the DNS system was introduced, allowing users to access resources through memorable URLs instead of raw IP addresses.

From the 1990s onward came the age of the public Internet. With the worldwide release of the World Wide Web in 1991, the Internet evolved from a semi-closed research network into a global public network. The rise of web browsers and ISPs then accelerated its commercialization and mass adoption.

[Timeline of Computer Development](https://postimg.cc/nXgWqP5R)

From one command, one program, one terminal, and one local network to a global Internet, the development and diffusion of computer networking has been faster than that of almost any other technology.

## Protocols and Ports / Interfaces

When processing information, we may imagine the computer as a strict and perfectionist English professor who refuses to accept broken grammar or informal phrasing. Even if a phrase like “Long time no see” is understandable to an ordinary native speaker, this professor insists that language conveys information, and that correct grammar is necessary for correct interpretation. In the same way, components in a computer system must communicate according to precise “grammar,” namely **protocols**. For example, all information transmission must conform to packet-based transmission protocols. What this professor calls “correct” really means **unique and unambiguous**: no dialects, no fuzziness. English should be Oxford English, and Internet communication should comply with internationally standardized models such as OSI.

Computer networking is efficient and compact. Suppose I want to send the sentence “Hello world” to a classmate in California. If I do not alter the information itself, I could only write it down and mail it physically. Network communication can instead be understood as repeatedly **translating** the information: wrapping the original message inside a package, attaching additional translated information to that package, and then repeating the process until everything is encoded purely in 0s and 1s. Because this language is simple, it can be carried by many physical media, such as high/low voltage levels or the presence/absence of light. In any case, each stage of translation corresponds to a **layer**. Communication **within** a layer follows protocols, while communication **between** layers relies on interfaces.

## The OSI Layered Model

[OSI model](https://postimg.cc/Jt0bWztX)

Let us take Baidu Search as an example and examine the most important layers in the OSI model and the protocols they use.

When you try to search something on Baidu, you first enter its URL. At this point, you are directly interacting with the **application layer**, which follows the HTTPS protocol (HyperText Transfer Protocol Secure). Hypertext is the structural language used to build browser pages; on macOS you can often inspect it in the browser via developer tools. Other protocols at the same layer include TELNET, SMTP, HTML, and SSL. The purpose of this layer is to ensure that the web UI is displayed properly and that the browser **functions normally as an application**.

The next important layer is the **transport layer**, which follows the TCP protocol (Transmission Control Protocol). TCP is connection-oriented. ICMP (used by the `ping` command) is another transport/network-related protocol often discussed in basic networking contexts. Another major transport-layer protocol is UDP, which is connectionless and is often used in scenarios such as video transmission. UDP is fast, but unordered, unreliable, and potentially lossy. The famous **three-way handshake** of TCP is as follows:

**Step 1**: The client sends a packet with the SYN flag to the server, indicating that it wants to establish a connection and providing an initial sequence number.  
**Step 2**: The server replies with a packet carrying both SYN and ACK flags, confirming the client’s SYN and supplying its own initial sequence number.  
**Step 3**: The client responds with an ACK packet acknowledging the server’s sequence number, and the connection is established.

The purpose of this layer is to ensure the **reliability, safety, and integrity of data transmission**.

Then comes the **network layer**, which follows the IP protocol. The Baidu URL you entered is resolved into an IP address by a **DNS** server or a local DNS configuration. What people often call “DNS poisoning,” one of the reasons some users on the mainland rely on VPNs, is precisely a problem of failing to resolve the desired IP address correctly. IP is a kind of **logical address**. It is unique within a given **subnet**, but not globally unique — for example, the loopback IP address `127.0.0.1` is used by all local machines. The purpose of this layer is to make addressing possible and to ensure, at the hardware-network level, that information can be delivered to the correct destination.

Next is the **data link layer**, which uses MAC addressing. Unlike IP, a MAC address is often called a **physical address**. It is a “hard” address rather than a “soft” one. MAC addresses are unique, and devices on the same subnet can discover one another’s MAC addresses, for example via `arp -a` in the terminal. **ARP** serves a role analogous to DNS, except that it resolves IP addresses into MAC addresses. The purpose of this layer is to handle **the creation and reception of data frames between directly connected nodes at the physical interconnection level**.

In other words, as data descends toward the physical layer, each layer adds its own protocol-compliant “shipping label” to the packet header, so that no matter which “courier company” or “station” handles the packet around the world, it can still be routed in the correct direction. Data transmission is, in essence, a cycle of **encapsulating a package and then unpacking it**.

The final layer is the **physical layer**, where everything is ultimately reduced to the two states 0 and 1. The purpose of this layer is to ensure **bitstream transmission and the conversion between electrical/optical signals and bits**.

Then, when my request reaches Baidu’s servers, the response is packaged in a similar way and sent back to my address.

## Monitoring Traffic on Your Local Device

[Wireshark](https://www.wireshark.org/) is a tool that can monitor data traffic on your computer. By right-clicking a frame and choosing “copy bytes as hex + ASCII dump,” then sending the dump to GPT for analysis, even a beginner in computer systems can start to understand why a request returned 404, why packets were lost, and so on.
