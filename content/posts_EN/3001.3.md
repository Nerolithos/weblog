## Ordinary Induction

<br>

To prove P(x) for all x ‚Č• n:
Base case: compute P(n) directly and show that it is true.
Induction step: **assume P(m) is true** and derive that P(m+1) is true.

Example: prove the Cauchy inequality (for arbitrary a<sub>i</sub>, b<sub>i</sub>)
$$a_{1}b_{1} + a_{2}b_{2} + \cdots + a_{n}b_{n} \;\leq\; \sqrt{a_{1}^{2} + a_{2}^{2} + \cdots + a_{n}^{2}} \; \sqrt{b_{1}^{2} + b_{2}^{2} + \cdots + b_{n}^{2}}\ \ \ \ \ (n)$$
$$Proof:\ Base\ case:|a_1b_1| = \sqrt{a_1^2}\sqrt{b_1^2},\ \ so\ a_1b_1 ‚Č§ \sqrt{a_1^2}\sqrt{b_1^2}\ \ \ \ \ (1)$$
$$Induction:Given\ (n),\ denote\ it\ as\ \ x‚Č§pq,\ so:$$
$$x+a_{n+1}b_{n+1}‚Č§pq+a_{n+1}b_{n+1}$$
$$Given\ (2),\ pq+a_{n+1}b_{n+1}‚Č§\sqrt{p^2+a_{n+1}^2}\sqrt{q^2+b_{n+1}^2}\ \ \ \ \ (n+1)\ \ Q.E.D$$
(Because the recursion implicitly uses the fact that the inequality holds for two terms, **one must additionally verify the two-term case**.)

## Strong Induction

<br>

To prove P(x) for all x ‚Č• n:
Base case: compute P(n) directly and show that it is true.
Induction step: **assume P(n), ..., P(m) are all true**, and derive that P(m+1) is true.

Example: prove that every integer greater than 8 can be formed using additions of only 3 and 5.
List the first few cases: 3+5=8, 3+3+3=9, 5+5=10.
For n = 11, we have n-3 = 8, which is representable.

## The Well-Ordering Principle (WOP)

<br>

$ WOP: every nonempty set of natural numbers has a least element.
Equivalent form: every nonempty set of positive integers has a least element.

To prove P(x) for all x ‚ąą N:
Assume ¬¨P(x), and let S ::= {n ‚ąą N | ¬¨P(n)}. By WOP, S has a least element.
Then derive some property of this least element that forces the existence of an even smaller counterexample, yielding a contradiction.

## Invariant Method

<br>

Given a sequence of integers (say, the integers from 1 to 100), each move removes two numbers and inserts the sum of their squares. Is the final remaining number fixed? Will it be odd or even?

**Key idea: the overall parity is an invariant.**
The magnitude is not fixed, because the returned sum of squares may itself be chosen again later. However, regardless of which two numbers are removed, the parity of the sum of their squares is always equal to the parity of their sum. Therefore, the parity of the final number must equal the parity of the sum of all original numbers, which is even.
