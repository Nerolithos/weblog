牧羊人的故事

October 30, 2024

牧羊人是个精明人，他总是能从羊群身上获得最多价值。冬天，羊长出很多毛，他就把大半的毛都剃掉，薄利多销地卖与人。因为价格实惠，人们都赞不绝口，经常从他那里进货。但牧羊人告诉羊群这些毛是为了实现羊群富足的必要牺牲，他永远以羊群利益至上。之所以要把毛交给牧羊人是因为他是羊群的主心骨，带领羊群走向温饱。

牧羊人自知自己很贪婪，每次都忍不住要把羊毛剃光，导致很多羊都冻死。但他不肯减少收入，就告诉羊它们同类的死因是自然灾害，叫羊群不要 “自己不努力生存就躺平，反倒怪罪无辜的牧羊人”。后来有些羊看见一群人穿着毛线衫，每天都能换一种色，就试图捍卫自己的毛。牧羊人见状立即开着卖羊毛换来的车把反抗的羊轧死，剁成碎末，喂给一部分精壮的羊，那些羊吃了同类竟然变幻身形化成了犬，牧羊人很高兴，让它们替自己牧羊。这些牧羊犬颐指气使，狐假虎威，宣扬反对人类就是反对羊群。真正的敌人是山羊，它们竟然认为羊应该有权控制自己的毛，简直是自私的吸血鬼。而牧羊人是伟大的，他让人类先暖起来，从而让人暖带动羊暖。牧羊犬以为自己既然与牧羊人分食羊肉，就属于牧羊人的同类。哪知一天牧羊人的大儿子得糖尿病了，他一夜杀光所有牧羊犬，竟想要用狗的肝和肾来救活自己的孩子。他的孩子终究死了，因为没有任何 “动物” 是不死的。可高傲的牧羊人竟认为自己不归自然规则所管辖，好似高于别的动物一般。

后来有些羊逃出了羊圈，就变成了山羊。牧羊人说，那些可恶的独立分子被自己身上太多的毛重的压死了，这就是为什么牧羊人以后要剪去羊身上过多的毛，替羊群批判性使用羊毛。羊群听了更拥护牧羊人了，他们一个个都觉得自己仿佛也化为了那牧羊犬。

之后，失去羊毛的羊群为了抱团取暖，生出更多的羊。羊越来越多，吃了牧羊人太多食物，又让他很难来得及把羊毛剃完，这让牧羊人的收益下降。他宣布限制两头羊只能繁殖一个小羊，并宣传这显著提升了羊均生产值，让羊类实现了伟大复兴。不久后，羊的数量急剧下降，牧羊人的羊毛储量又不够了，他便怪罪羊太懒惰才会让毛减少。他鼓励羊多繁衍小羊，把羊肉奖励给多产子的羊。这般往复，羊群被折腾的越发少，但牧羊人仍然控制不住贪婪，每次都要把毛全剃光。最终，羊群在寒风中死绝了。虽然羊圈旁边就是牧羊人烧着羊毛供暖的住所，也没有羊胆敢走进去，它们至死还认为这是万恶的山羊导致的。

至于牧羊人，他卷着羊毛和换来的钱重新做起了种植韭菜的生意，他依旧过得奢侈又幸福。牧羊人晚年被采访时总结说：世界上有两种看法——垂叶说和丛林说，都不大正确，前者认为雨水会从高大的植物的叶子上漏下一部分滋养底部的植物，而后者认为虽然雨水不会落到地面但低矮的植物也不会死绝。而我认为应当折中，只要控制的好，不管是羊群还是韭菜，给他们足够的 “雨水” 让他们处于苟且偷生但不至于灭亡的边缘，他们就会言听计从，拱手奉上所有生产成果，甚至互相陷害算计用来谄媚我。多么精明的牧羊人。


The shepherd was a shrewd man. He always knew how to extract the greatest value from his flock.

In winter, when the sheep grew thick coats of wool, he would shear off most of it and sell it cheaply in large quantities. Because the price was attractive, people praised him highly and often bought from him.

But the shepherd told the flock that giving up their wool was a necessary sacrifice for the prosperity of the sheep. He claimed that he always put the flock’s interests first. The wool had to be handed over to him, he said, because he was the backbone of the flock, the one who would lead them toward warmth and plenty.

The shepherd knew full well that he was greedy. Every time, he could not help shearing the sheep completely bare, causing many of them to freeze to death. But he refused to reduce his income. So he told the sheep that the deaths of their own kind were caused by natural disasters, and warned them not to “refuse to work hard to survive, lie flat, and then blame the innocent shepherd.”

Later, some sheep saw people wearing wool sweaters, changing into a different one every day, and began trying to defend their own wool. Seeing this, the shepherd immediately drove the car he had bought with the proceeds from selling wool and ran over the rebellious sheep. He chopped them into pieces and fed them to some of the stronger sheep.

After eating their own kind, those sheep transformed in shape and became dogs. The shepherd was delighted, and ordered them to herd the flock on his behalf.

These sheepdogs became arrogant and domineering, borrowing the shepherd’s authority to intimidate others. They proclaimed that opposing humans was the same as opposing the flock. The real enemy, they said, was the mountain goats, who actually believed that sheep should have the right to control their own wool. How selfish and parasitic of them.

The shepherd, by contrast, was great. He had first made humans warm, and through human warmth, sheep warmth would eventually be achieved.

The sheepdogs believed that because they shared sheep meat with the shepherd, they were now of the same kind as him. But one day, the shepherd’s eldest son developed diabetes. Overnight, the shepherd slaughtered all the sheepdogs, hoping to use their livers and kidneys to save his child.

His child died in the end, because no “animal” is immortal. Yet the proud shepherd believed that he himself was exempt from the laws of nature, as though he stood above all other animals.

Later, some sheep escaped the pen and became mountain goats. The shepherd said that those hateful separatists had been crushed to death by the excessive weight of their own wool. That, he explained, was precisely why he had to shear away the sheep’s surplus wool in the future, so that he could help the sheeps “dispose of” the wool.

After hearing this, the flock supported the shepherd even more. One by one, they felt as if they too had become sheepdogs.

Afterward, the woolless sheep, desperate to huddle together for warmth, gave birth to more sheep. The number of sheep grew larger and larger. They ate too much of the shepherd’s food, and made it difficult for him to shear all their wool in time. This caused the shepherd’s profits to decline.

So he announced that two sheep would only be allowed to produce one lamb. He proclaimed that this policy had greatly increased per-sheep productivity and allowed sheepkind to achieve a great rejuvenation.

Before long, the sheep population fell sharply. The shepherd’s wool reserves became insufficient again. He then blamed the sheep for being too lazy, saying that their laziness had caused the supply of wool to decline.

He encouraged the sheep to reproduce more lambs, rewarding those who bore more offspring with sheep meat.

Again and again this cycle repeated itself. The flock was tossed about until it dwindled further and further. Yet the shepherd still could not control his greed. Every time, he had to shear all the wool off completely.

In the end, the flock died out in the freezing wind.

Although right beside the sheepfold stood the shepherd’s house, warmed by burning sheep wool, not a single sheep dared to enter it. Until their deaths, they still believed that everything had been caused by the evil mountain goats.

As for the shepherd, he took the wool and the money he had made, and started a new business growing chives. He continued to live extravagantly and happily.

In his later years, when interviewed, the shepherd summarized his philosophy:

“There are two views in the world: the trickle-down theory and the jungle theory. Neither is quite correct. The former believes that rainwater will drip down from the leaves of tall plants and nourish the plants below. The latter believes that although rainwater will not reach the ground, the low-growing plants will not die out completely either.

I believe in a middle path.

As long as things are controlled properly, whether they are sheep or chives, if you give them just enough ‘rainwater’ to keep them barely alive but never truly secure, they will obey you, hand over all the fruits of their labor, and even scheme against one another in order to flatter you.

What a shrewd shepherd.”
